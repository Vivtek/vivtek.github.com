This is a little story I wrote in a single swoop in college, that is, sometime between 1984 and 1988, but probably in 1986.I wrote it on my Apple ][e and printed it on a real live dot-matrix printer to show my mother.And it's a good thing I did; I sold the Apple some years later and the disks are long gone even if I could figure out how to read them.I ran across the paper copy in my mother's magazine rack in the early 90's and took it home with me to type, filed it, and forgot about it completely.

 

Fast-forward to 2006, and we have a dumpster out front while we replace a bathroom. My wife instructs me to sort through this old rusty filing cabinet with all the crap in it, so we can toss the cabinet while we still have the dumpster available.I find the long-lost ultrasound pics of our daughter, and this hard copy of my very first story.

 

I read the story.  And it is good. So good I'll resist editing it at all while typing it, for historical interest if for no other reason. Apologies for the odd formatting; I type in Word, and for various technical reasons, I've been using Word 2000 lately, and it doesn't render to HTML very well. I may fix it all up later, but in the meantime, eh, it's readable. Live with it.

 

THE DRAGON: Installment the First

The dragon had no name.It had once had a name, far in the past, but it had long since been forgotten. Now it was simply "the dragon."  Nothing further was necessary.

Over the eons of its slow, contented life, it had amassed quite a hoard of diverse shiny things: jewels, gold, pretty rocks; anything that happened to have caught its eye.There were two reasons for this: first, it is any dragon's purpose in life to collect thing; second, no-one really cares to oppose a dragon.

Another thing dragons are noted for is their magical abilities. This dragon, no slouch in preserving the status quo, had also collected all manner of flashy spells – in a word, anything that caught its eye.Naturally, it hadn't created any of its own; that is not in a dragon's nature.  Dragons are collectors, and none too organized in that capacity.

This dragon, then, was quite an average dragon, except for its age – it had been around a very long time, long enough that it had itself forgotten the exact time of its beginnings.And as for the inhabitants of the surrounding countryside, they considered it a fixture.It had been around long before the settling of the region; perhaps even before its discovery – no-one knew any more.It had seen the land develop from untracked wilderness, to a sleepy rural area, to a thriving net of commerce.

It was a comfortable life.The hours were good, the neighbors pleasant. The neighbors, in fact, were quite helpful in fulfilling the dragon's desires regarding collecting.They would often bring him nice new objects, simply asking information in return, for the dragon (naturally) collected knowledge as well.

It was a comfortable life.

 

John Morrison's life, however, was anything but comfortable. The hours were impossible, and he tried not to think of the neighbors.John was the sole salesman for a rapidly sinking insurance company. This in itself should not have been so bad, but for the fact that the only reason he held this job was to pay for a university education.John was working his way through school at a small Midwestern engineering college, and jobs were few and far between.

Each day after classes, John would put in five hours (minimum) at the office, then work out for an hour, do some homework, and collapse into bed for a meager amount of sleep before starting the cycle over.

Needless to say, the pressure of this boringly repetitive lifestyle was slowly driving him to insanity.And his neighbors, as I said earlier, were less than desirable. They had moved in just a week or two ago, but it seemed like a year.At all hours of the night, there were all sorts of sounds coming from the house, sometime horrendous sounds like the screams of cats, sometimes just bangs, as from heavy things falling.  There were already many rumors flying around about these people.Some said they were escaped criminals.Others maintained they were a family of hereditary psychopaths. Some swore up and down they were haunted somehow.

Some, a small number, but some, thought they were druids.

They were right.

 

The druids play a pivotal role in this tale, because they were of a rather experimental nature.The moment they discovered something druidic, they had to try it out, often on some unsuspecting soul that happened to be walking by their humble abode.  They had done many strange and horrible things.They had done a small number of really neat and wonderful things. As all knowledge, druidic knowledge can be used for any purpose, good or evil.These druids weren't using it; they were just trying (rather ineptly) to discover it.

At any rate, they were just about to try another thing that had captured their fickle attention – dimensional travel. They had recently happened upon extremely ancient excerpts from books on the subject, and one instruction booklet for operating a certain druidic item that allowed inter-universal movement. Unfortunately, they had no actual theoretical knowledge, and so they had been trying to get the process right for some time, without astounding success.However, as you might guess, they had perfected the knowledge just before this story begins. And, also rather predictably, they haphazardly chose our friend John Morrison as their experimental subject.

John's life was at least going to lose its boring repetitiveness.

So was the dragon's.

 

Thus ends Chapter One, being just over one printed page of Apple ][e text. The story will continue when I have more time to type it.
