Where did my Google go?
Michael Roberts
michael@vivtek.com

Focus

Since my debut as an independent software designer/programmer, my resumé has gotten a little messy.

However, after five years independent consulting, I think I know what it is I do, basically: whatever people pay me to do. In practice, this is mostly the development of online databases and other active-content sites. I'm getting halfway decent at layout, and this interfaces well with my strong database skills.

You can get an idea of my experience either by keyword or in a more traditional chronological list. The disadvantage of the chronological list is that so many things overlap in the last few years. However, it's interesting from a historical perspective; the laundry list below only contains skills in which I consider myself current.

Technical experience by keyword

  • Workflow
    I am the instigator and maintainer of the open-source workflow toolkit wftk. This has been going on for two years so far, and I'm really just now getting to what I consider my first production release. But it's powerful stuff.
  • Database systems
    • MySQL
    • PostgreSQL
    • Oracle
    • Access and ODBC
  • Web servers/application servers
    • AOLserver
    • Apache
    • Zope
    • PHP
  • Languages
    • C/C++
    • Perl
    • Python
  • Platforms
    • Solaris
    • Linux
    • Windows

Sole proprietor of Vivtek. (http://www.vivtek.com)

May 2000 - Jan 2001:
Developer with HotDispatch.
HotDispatch paid me for 4 hours of every day. I did general utility development, consulted on usability, help analyze traffic, and other stuff that fit around the edges of their full-time development staff. I liked it, they liked it -- it's amazing how well it worked out, really. Then they grew from 22 to 45 people in six weeks, and decided to concentrate management on the local staff. Made sense to me.
Technologies: Perl/DBI, LISP (if you can imagine that), Java, XML, lots of other laundry-list keywords.

I was their only part-time employee, though, and we all found it simply too difficult to plan around that. So we parted ways. It wasn't an easy decision.
